Data-Driven Offline Optimization of Deep CNN
models for EEG and ECoG Decoding
Antonios Tragoudaras, Konstantinos Fanaras, Charalampos Antoniadis and Yehia Massoud
Innovative Technologies Laboratories (ITL),
King Abdullah University of Science and Technology (KAUST), Thuwal, Saudi Arabia
{antonios.tragoudaras, charalampos.antoniadis, yehia.massoud}@kaust.edu.sa
Abstract—A better understanding of ElectroEncephaloGra-
phy (EEG) and ElectroCorticoGram (ECoG) signals would
get us closer to comprehending brain functionality, creating
new avenues for treating brain abnormalities and developing
novel Brain-Computer Interface (BCI)-related applications. Deep
Convolutional Neural Networks (deep CNNs) have lately been
employed with remarkable success to decode EEG/ECoG signals.
However, the optimal architectural/training parameter values
in these deep CNN architectures have received little attention.
In addition, new data-driven optimization methodologies that
leverage significant advancements in Machine Learning, such as
the Transformer model, have recently been proposed. Because an
exhaustive search on all possible architectural/training parameter
values of the state-of-the-art deep CNN model (our baseline
model) decoding the motor imagery EEG and finger tension
ECoG signals comprising the BCI IV 2a and 4 datasets, respec-
tively, would require prohibitively much time, this paper proposes
a model-based optimization technique based on the Transformer
model for the discovery of the optimal architectural/training
parameter values for that model. Our findings indicate that we
could pick better values for the architectural/training parameters
of the baseline model, enhancing the accuracy of the baseline
model by 3.4% in the BCI IV 2a dataset and by 29.8% in the
BCI IV 4 dataset.
Index Terms—CNN, EEG Signals, NAS, BCIC IV 2a and 4
datasets
I. INTRODUCTION
The extraction of relevant information from EEG recordings
using deep Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) techniques
has demonstrated promising results in detecting neurological
illnesses such as epilepsy [1] and its treatment feasibility
[2], identifying sleep disorders [3], and supporting stroke
rehabilitation via Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) [4], [5].
However, despite the tremendous advances in CNN-assisted
EEG analysis, optimizing the EEG-decoding-CNN models’
parameters has received little attention. Because we cannot
derive a closed-form formula for the evaluation of a deep
CNN model (we say that we consider it as a ” black box”),
and on top of that, the evaluation of a deep CNN model is
time-consuming because it involves the model training/test on
given training/test sets, offline model-based optimization is
an attractive method for optimizing the EEG-decoding-CNN
models architectural/training parameters.
In offline model-based optimization, a surrogate model of a
complex system 1 (like the evaluation of deep CNN consisting
of many parameters which in turn take many possible values)
is first derived and then used as a substitute for the original
1A system that requires a long time to respond at a given input and the
input space is gigantic.
system (of the deep CNN model evaluation), usually in an
evolutionary algorithm that performs the optimization. As a
result, the optimization needs less time, or the evolutionary
algorithm can make a more extensive search space exploration,
arriving at better solutions. Offline model-based optimization
has proven very effective in biology problems [6]–[8]. At the
same time, the latest advancement in Machine Learning (ML),
such as the Transformer model, has raised the idea of building
an offline data-driven surrogate model for optimizing hardware
accelerators [9].
In this paper, we exploit a data-driven offline optimization
technique based on the Transformer model for building the
surrogate model. Using that surrogate model, we optimize
the state-of-the-art deep CNN model decoding the motor
imagery EEG signals in the BCI IV 2a datasets [10] and the
finger tension ECoG signals in the BCI IV 4 dataset [11].
In order to do that, we first perform a random sampling
on the baseline model architectural/training parameters space
under consideration to create a dataset that matches these
random architectural/training parameter values to the evaluated
accuracy of the baseline model. Then, we provide that dataset
to the proposed model-based optimization methodology to find
the architectural/training parameter values that achieve the best
accuracy.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows: Section II
describes the baseline deep CNN model architecture and
the motor imagery EEG and finger tension ECoG signals
datasets. Section III explains the proposed data-driven offline
optimization methodology to find the best values for the
baseline CNN model architectural/training parameters. Finally,
in section IV, we demonstrate the better accuracy we can
obtain using the architectural/training values found by the
proposed optimization methodology, while conclusions are
drawn in Section V.
II. MOTOR IMAGERY EEG/ECOG SIGNALS DECODING
The authors of [12] demonstrated a variety of CNN architec-
tures, ranging from 2- to 31-layer CNN networks, for decoding
the EEG signals in the motor imagery BCI IV 2a dataset.
Furthermore, these deep CNN models could decode motor
imagery EEG signals data as well as or better than Filter Bank
Common Spatial Patterns (FBCSP) [13], the winner of many
EEG-decoding competitions. Finally, the Shallow ConvNet
model was the most accurate on average for all subjects among
all the proposed architectures in that work.

A. BCI IV 2a dataset
The 22-electrode EEG recordings comprising the BCI IV
2a dataset represent nine patients’ motor imagery-related
brain activity. Each individual i has a distinct collection of
EEG recordings made while performing four distinct body
movements, namely ”Left Hand”, ”Right Hand”, ”Feet” and
”Rest”. The j-th entry of the i-th subject in the dataset is
represented as Di = {Xj, Y j}, j from 1 to the total number
of recorded trials, where Xj ∈ℜE×T and Y j ∈ℜT , with E
denoting the number of recorded electrodes, T the number of
timesteps after the discretization of each trial, and Y j being
labeled with one of the four types of body movements, in each
timestep of the j-th trial.
B. BCI IV 4 dataset
The shallow ConvNet model has also been tested on Electro-
physiological (ECoG) signals in the BCI IV 4 dataset showing
better performance than previous works.
The BCI IV 4 dataset consists of 62, 48, and 64 -electrode
ECoG recordings corresponding to 3 epileptic patients. The
dataset has the same structure as the BCI IV 2a. However,
measurements of finger flexion (continuous values) are given
at every timestep instead of class labels for every trial.
C. Shallow ConvNet
Shallow ConvNet mimics the spatial filters (bandpass and
Common Spatial Patterns (CSP)) in FBCSP with temporal
and spatial convolution in the initial two layers. A squaring
nonlinearity layer, a mean pooling layer, and a dense layer
with the softmax activation function comprise the remainder
of the pipeline, which resembles the logarithmic variance
calculation in FBCSP. Figure 1 illustrates the Shallow ConvNet
model architecture. Although Shallow ConvNet is accurate
enough, its accuracy can be improved further by tuning its
architectural/training parameters.
III. DATA-DRIVEN OPTIMIZATION OF THE SHALLOW
CONVNET ARCHITECTURE
To improve the Shallow ConvNet architecture, we alter
some of the Shallow ConvNet architectural/training parame-
ters. First, we assume a vast search space consisting of 8.5
billion distinct architectural/training configurations to exhaust
our options for finding a more accurate Shallow ConvNet-like
model. Then, using a small sample of Shallow ConvNet-like
modified models, D = {{x1, y1}, .., {xi, yi}, ..., {xn, yn}},
with xi being a Shallow ConvNet architecture with random
architectural/training parameters values, and yi being the ac-
curacy of that model after training and testing it on a given
training/test set, we train a surrogate model, based on the
Transformer model, predicting the accuracy of new Shallow
ConvNet architectures. Finally, we employ the Firefly evolu-
tionary optimization algorithm using the previous surrogate
model to explore the Shallow ConvNet architectural/training
space and discover their best values that maximize the accu-
racy of the Shallow ConvNet model [12].

IV. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
In this section, we demonstrate that the Firefly Optimizer
using as objective the surrogate model (explained in subsection
III-B) that can predict the accuracy of the Shallow ConvNet
model for different sets of its architectural/training parameter
values could discover a more accurate Shallow ConvNet model
on the BCI IV 2a and 4 datasets.
Our experimental framework was developed in Python using
the TensorFlow library. All our experiments were performed
on a system with a single NVIDIA V100 with 64GB of RAM
and a 3.7GHz Intel Xeon Gold 6154 CPU.

A. Training configurations
First, we created a dataset consisting of 8000 ConvNet
architectures, each with a different combination of the archi-
tectural/training parameter values we consider in this work (by
random sampling the Shallow ConvNet architectural/training
parameters search space) and their corresponding average
accuracy for all patients in the BCI IV 2a and 4 datasets,
respectively. We then provided these two datasets to Algorithm
1 to train the surrogate model for 60K gradient steps. On top
of that, we considered the following training strategies for the
train/validation split during our experiments. Either train on
80% of the worst-performing configurations and validate on
the 20% best-performing configurations, or train on 90% of
the worst-performing configurations and validate on 10% of
best-performing configurations.

V. CONCLUSION
This paper determines the ideal architectural/training pa-
rameter values for the Shallow ConvNet model, the state-
of-the-art deep CNN (our baseline model) for decoding the
motor imagery EEG/ECoG signals constituting the BCI IV 2a
and 4 datasets. Our findings demonstrate that we can choose
better settings for the Shallow ConvNet architectural/training
parameters that we consider for optimization in this work,
increasing the baseline model’s accuracy by 3.4% and 29.8%
in BCI IV 2a and 4 datasets, respectively.
