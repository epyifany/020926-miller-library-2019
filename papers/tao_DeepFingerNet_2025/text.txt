DeepFingerNet Predicts Finger Trajectory
From ECoG Measurements
Wei Tao , Jianghao Hou , Yi Yang , Xun Chen , Senior Member, IEEE, Tzyy-Ping Jung , Fellow, IEEE,
and Feng Wan , Senior Member, IEEE
Abstract—Decoding ﬁne motor movements, such as ﬁnger
trajectories, from brain signals poses a critical challenge for
brain-computer
interface
(BCI)
systems,
particularly
when
employing electrocorticography (ECoG) because of its inher-
ent signal complexity and inter-subject variability. Although
traditional machine learning and deep learning methods have
advanced coarse motor control, they often fall short in captur-
ing and generalizing. To address these limitations, we propose
DeepFingerNet—a novel architecture based on nested U-Nets
designed speciﬁcally for ECoG-based ﬁnger trajectory predic-
tion. DeepFingerNet enhances feature extraction and fusion by
leveraging nested skip connections, which eﬀectively integrate
low-level spatial details with high-level abstract representations.
We employ a hybrid loss function, combining cosine similarity
and mean squared error (mse), to optimize the direction and mag-
nitude of the decoded trajectories. Evaluations on two publicly
available ECoG datasets demonstrate the model’s eﬀectiveness,
achieving correlation coeﬃcients of 0.69 (a 17% improvement
over baseline methods) on BCI Competition IV Dataset 4 and
0.54 (a 5% improvement) on the Stanford University dataset,
respectively. These results underscore DeepFingerNet’s potential
to signiﬁcantly improve the reliability and precision of ﬁne
ﬁnger predictions from ECoG measurements, thereby supporting
advanced applications in neuroprosthetics and human–machine
interaction.
Index Terms—Deep learning, electrocorticography (ECoG),
ﬁnger trajectory decoding, real-time brain-computer interface
(BCI), U-Nets.

II. RELATED WORK
A. ECoG for Motor Decoding
BCIs play a vital role in enabling direct neural control
for motor restoration [2], emotion regulation [23], [24], [25],
and communication enhancement [26]. ECoG-based motor
decoding has gained signiﬁcant attention for its potential in
BCIs because of its superior spatial and temporal resolution
compared to EEG. Early work by Penﬁeld and Jasper [27]
(1950’s) established the foundation for brain mapping, while
Schalk et al. [28] demonstrated ECoG’s application in 2-D
cursor control, achieving accuracy rates of 53%–73%. Subse-
quently, Flint et al. [29], and Warren et al. [30] showcased
ECoG’s potential in prosthetic control and grasping force
decoding. However, traditional methods using handcrafted
features like spectral power and phase-amplitude coupling,
classiﬁed with models such as SVM, LDA, and KNN, strug-
gle with ﬁne-grained tasks like ﬁnger trajectory decoding.
Methods such as Flamary and Rakotomamonjy [19] switched
linear models and Yao et al.’s [17] time-frequency extraction
achieved moderate success but failed to capture the temporal
dynamics critical for decoding subtle motor movements.
Recent advances in deep learning, especially CNNs com-
bined with LSTMs, have signiﬁcantly improved the ability to
decode ﬁnger movements from ECoG signals. Xie et al. [20]
demonstrated that integrating CNNs with LSTMs produces
smoother and more accurate ﬁnger trajectory predictions. Sim-
ilarly, Lomtev et al. [21] proposed the FingerFlex model, while
Kuo et al. [22] developed a 3D-CNN model that outperformed
traditional methods, highlighting the critical role of the high
gamma band in ﬁnger movement decoding. These innovations
underscore the shift toward deep learning models for ECoG
decoding, which more eﬀectively capture the spatial and tem-
poral complexities of high-dimensional data, paving the way
for advancements in neuroprosthetics and motor rehabilitation.
B. U-Net in Brain–Computer Interfaces
The U-Net architecture, originally developed for medical
image segmentation [31], has been successfully adapted for
BCIs, particularly in analyzing EEG and ECoG signals [32],
[33], [34]. Its ability to capture spatial and temporal features
makes it well-suited for decoding motor intentions and cogni-
tive states requiring high-resolution signal analysis.
In early applications, U-Net was used for EEG signal clas-
siﬁcation, demonstrating its eﬀectiveness in learning relevant
features from time-series data. Khessiba et al. [33] developed
two UNet-based deep learning architectures (1D-UNet and
1D-UNet-LSTM) for detecting alertness states, outperform-
ing existing methods in accuracy and recall, with real-time
performance on devices like Raspberry Pi 3. Similarly,
Chatzichristos et al. [35] proposed a multiview attention-
gated U-Net for seizure detection, enhancing performance
by fusing EEG data from diﬀerent preprocessing techniques
and incorporating a bidirectional LSTM to capture long-term
dependencies. Additionally, U-Net has been applied to remove
EEG artifacts. Chuang et al. [34] introduced IC-U-Net, which
eﬀectively combines independent component analysis (ICA)
with U-Net to remove artifacts (e.g., eye movements, muscle

III. MATERIALS AND METHODS
A. Dataset Description
This study uses two publicly available datasets to evaluate
the proposed method: the BCI Competition IV Dataset 41 [36]
and a public dataset provided by Stanford University2 [37].
These datasets collectively include data from 12 subjects, with
three subjects from the BCI Competition IV Dataset 4 and
nine subjects from the Stanford dataset. Both datasets feature
participants with epilepsy.
Both datasets follow the same experimental paradigm.
A screen displaying a cue word was placed in front of each
subject. The subject was instructed to perform a single ﬁnger
movement in response to the cue word, with the subject
determining the frequency and amplitude of the movement.
Each cue word lasted for two seconds, followed by a two-
second rest period before presenting the next.
The ECoG data were recorded from 48 to 64 electrodes,
distributed across grids such as 6 × 8 or 8 × 8, with the
number and distribution of electrodes varying across subjects.
Finger movement data were captured using a glove placed on
the subject’s hand, which recorded the amplitude of the ﬁnger
movement. The initial ECoG data had a sampling frequency
of 1000 Hz, while the ﬁnger movement data were sampled at
25 Hz.
B. Preprocessing and Feature Extraction
The preprocessing pipeline for both datasets follows a
consistent procedure, as they share the same experimental
paradigm and data frequency [21]. Preprocessing comprises
three stages: ECoG data preprocessing, ﬁnger movement data
preprocessing, and data clipping, as shown in Fig. 1.
The signals are ﬁrst standardized for the ECoG data prepro-
cessing by subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard
deviation for each channel. The median of each channel’s
signal is subtracted to enhance training convergence further.
We applied a bandpass ﬁlter of 40–300 Hz to the ECoG signals
to suppress low-frequency drift and motion artifacts while
preserving gamma and high-gamma oscillations that convey

C. Model Architecture
We propose DeepFingerNet, a deep learning model based
on the nested U-Nets++ architecture, for the challenging
task of decoding continuous ﬁnger trajectories from ECoG
signals. The U-Net serves as the foundation of DeepFin-
gerNet because it provides an eﬀective and interpretable
framework for modeling hierarchical neural representations
while preserving ﬁne-grained temporal–spatial information.
Given that ECoG signals exhibit complex dynamics across
multiple temporal and spectral scales, such a hierarchical
encoder–decoder structure is well suited to capture both
global contextual dependencies and local oscillatory features
essential for ﬁne-motor decoding. To further enhance feature
fusion, DeepFingerNet integrates nested skip connections,

D. Simulation Setup
In the BCI Competition IV Dataset 4, the training and
validation sets were predivided at a ratio of 6.5:3.5. Similarly,
the Stanford University public dataset was divided in the
same ratio for subsequent training. The training set was
used solely for model training and parameter optimization,
while the validation set served to prevent overﬁtting and was
used to calculate the correlation coeﬃcient for performance
evaluation.
We trained the model individually for each subject because
of diﬀerences in the number of ECoG signal channels and
electrode placement across subjects in the two datasets. To
improve the data utilization eﬃciency during training, we
adopted a sliding window approach to read the data succes-
sively. The window size was set to 256 time points (equivalent
to 2.56 seconds), with a sliding interval of 1 time point
(0.01 s). We set the training batch size to 128, using a
maximum of 20 epochs. The Adam optimizer was used, with
a learning rate of 2e-5 and a weight decay of 1e-6.
For the loss function, this work draws on the design of
FingerFlex [21], combining cosine similarity and mse. Cosine
similarity focuses on the angular distance between the output
vector and the target vector, optimizing for direction but
ignoring size diﬀerences. In contrast, mse emphasizes the
magnitude diﬀerence between the output and target vectors.
By combining both, we optimize the direction and magnitude
of the output vector. The ratio of cosine similarity to mse
in the loss function is 1:1, which is set to 0.5, respectively.
At the end of each epoch, the entire validation set was fed into
the model, and the decoded data was compared to the original
ﬁnger movement data to compute the correlation coeﬃcient.

assessment of the diﬀerences in performance.
IV. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
This section presents the detailed performance results of
DeepFingerNet, followed by an ablation study and an inter-
pretability analysis. Our results show that DeepFingerNet

data with higher-level abstract representations. The design
preserves spatial details while incorporating more global fea-

neurophysiological insights, highlighting the potential clinical
impact of DeepFingerNet in neuroprosthetics, rehabilitation,
and broader motor control applications.
V. DISCUSSION
Compared with prior CNN-based models, the novelty of
DeepFingerNet lies in its nested skip connections, which
provide dense feature fusion across multiple decoder depths.
This design preserves ﬁne temporal-spectral details while
progressively reﬁning high-level representations, an ability that
is especially beneﬁcial for continuous trajectory decoding.
Unlike attention or transformer-based approaches [49], [50],
which emphasize global dependencies but often require large-
scale training data, nested U-Net structures are well suited
tosuit limited neuroscience datasets, oﬀering a better bal-
ance between local precision, contextual integration, and data
eﬃciency.
The experimental results validate the eﬃcacy of DeepFin-
gerNet for decoding ﬁnger trajectories from ECoG signals. On
the BCI Competition IV dataset, DeepFingerNet achieved an
average correlation coeﬃcient of 0.69, outperforming other
state-of-the-art methods such as LSTM, Interpretable CNN,
LightGBM, and FingerFlex by up to 17%. Similarly, on
the Stanford dataset, the model yielded an average correla-
tion coeﬃcient of 0.54, demonstrating an improvement of
approximately 5% over traditional approaches such as Lin-
ear Regression and Band-Speciﬁc AM. These quantitative
improvements highlight the model’s capacity to accurately
capture the complex spatiotemporal dynamics inherent in
high-dimensional neural measurements.
Our ablation study conﬁrms that deeper nested U-Net
conﬁgurations yield superior decoding accuracy compared to
shallower models. Notably, the 3-UNet conﬁguration achieves
the highest average correlation across subjects, striking an

Despite the promising results, several limitations warrant
further discussion. The inherent variability of ECoG signals
across individuals still necessitates subject-speciﬁc calibration
or training, which may limit the scalability of DeepFingerNet

the nested U-Net architecture eﬀectively enhances decoding

cross-subject transferability, as well as model compression
and lightweight architectural designs to enhance eﬃciency.
Expanding evaluation to larger and more diverse clinical
datasets will also be essential to further validate the robustness
and generalizability of DeepFingerNet.
VI. CONCLUSION
In this work, we introduced DeepFingerNet, a novel nested
U-Nets-based framework for decoding ﬁne motor movements
from ECoG signals. By leveraging nested skip connections and
a hybrid loss function, DeepFingerNet eﬀectively fuses low-
level spatial details with high-level abstract representations,
yielding superior decoding accuracy for ﬁnger trajectory pre-
diction compared to baseline methods. Experimental results
on the BCI Competition IV Dataset 4 and the Stanford Uni-
versity dataset consistently demonstrated improved correlation
coeﬃcients over baseline methods, highlighting the model’s
robustness and eﬀectiveness in handling complex neural data.
Additionally, visualization of intermediate feature maps pro-
vided insights into the model’s internal processing, revealing
the progressive reﬁnement of neural features and enhancing
interpretability. These results not only demonstrate the model’s
potential for real-world BCI applications but also contribute to
advancing measurement accuracy and signal interpretation in
neural decoding systems. Future work will focus on improving
generalization across diverse subject populations and reducing
computational complexity to support deployment in resource-
constrained environments, further enhancing the practicality
and reliability of ECoG-based decoding for neuroprosthetic
control and motor rehabilitation.
