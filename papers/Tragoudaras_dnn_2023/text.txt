ABSTRACT A better understanding of ElectroEncephaloGraphy (EEG) and ElectroCorticoGram (ECoG)
signals would get us closer to comprehending brain functionality, creating new avenues for treating brain
abnormalities and developing novel Brain-Computer Interface (BCI)-related applications. Deep Neural
Networks (DNN)s have lately been employed with remarkable success to decode EEG/ECoG signals for
BCI. However, the optimal architectural/training parameter values in these DNN architectures have yet to
receive much attention. In addition, new data-driven optimization methodologies that leverage significant
advancements in Machine Learning, such as the Transformer model, have recently been proposed. Because
an exhaustive search on all possible architectural/training parameter values of the state-of-the-art DNN
model (our baseline model) decoding the motor imagery EEG and finger tension ECoG signals comprising
the BCI IV 2a and 4 datasets, respectively, would require prohibitively much time, this paper proposes an
offline model-based optimization technique based on the Transformer model for the discovery of the optimal
architectural/training parameter values for that model. Our findings indicate that we could pick better values
for the baseline model’s architectural/training parameters, enhancing the baseline model’s performance by
up to 14.7% in the BCI IV 2a dataset and by up to 61.0% in the BCI IV 4 dataset.

I. INTRODUCTION
ElectroEncephaloGraphy (EEG) records the brain’s sponta-
neous electrical activity as an electrogram. It is ordinarily
non-invasive, with electrodes inserted along the scalp (often
called ‘‘scalp EEG’’) with the International 10-20 system (see
Figure 1) or variants thereof. In addition, EleCtrocorticoG-
raphy (ECoG) is a form of electrophysiological monitoring
that records electrical activity from the cerebral cortex using
electrodes placed directly on the brain’s exposed surface.
Electrodes used in traditional EEG measure this activity from
outside the skull. On the other hand, because a craniotomy

However, despite the tremendous advances in deep NNs-
assisted EEG/ECoG analysis, optimizing the EEG/ECoG-
decoding-NN models’ parameters has yet to receive much
attention. Because we cannot derive a closed-form formula
for the evaluation of a deep NN model (we say that we con-
sider it as a ‘‘black box’’), and on top of that, the evaluation of
a deep NN model is time-consuming because it involves the
model training/test on given training/test sets, offline Model-
Based Optimization (MBO) is an attractive method for opti-
mizing the EEG-decoding-NN models architectural/training
parameters.
In offline MBO, a surrogate model of a complex system1
(like the evaluation of DNN consisting of many parameters
which in turn take many possible values) is first derived
and then used as a substitute for the original system (of the
DNN model evaluation), usually in an evolutionary algorithm
that performs the optimization. As a result, the optimization
needs less time, or the evolutionary algorithm can make a
more extensive search space exploration, arriving at better
solutions.
Offline MBO has proven very effective in biology prob-
lems [17], [18], [19]. However, all previous works mitigate
the wrong guidance given by the surrogate model to the
optimizer due to inaccurate predictions, more often overesti-
mations. That happens because the estimated model is trained
on a small manifold of the possible input space, making the
predictions far from the training distribution harder. More
specifically, [17] leverages a maximum-likelihood (NML)
estimator to reason about uncertainty and out-of-distribution
values and guide the optimizer to the best solution with
more confidence. In addition, [18] proposes a model-based
adaptive sampling, to sample with larger probability inputs
that may better optimize the desired properties during opti-
mization. Finally, [19] introduces the conservative objective
models (COM)s, i.e., a method that learns a model of the
objective function that lower bounds the actual value of the
ground-truth objective on out-of-distribution inputs and uses
it for optimization.
In this paper, we exploit a data-driven offline optimiza-
tion technique based on the Transformer model to optimize
the state-of-the-art deep CNN model decoding the motor
imagery EEG signals in the BCI IV 2a datasets [20] and the
finger tension ECoG signals in the BCI IV 4 dataset [21].
In order to do that, we first perform a random sampling on the
baseline model architectural/training parameters space under
consideration to create a dataset that matches these random
architectural/training parameter values to the evaluated accu-
racy of the baseline model. Then, we provide that dataset
to the proposed model-based optimization methodology that
first builds a surrogate model of the baseline model accuracy
given architectural/training parameter values and then makes
use of that surrogate model in a metaheuristic optimization
algorithm to find the architectural/training parameter values
that achieve the best accuracy. Our main contributions in this
work are:
1) Improve the accuracy of an existing Deep Learning
(DL) model for decoding EEG & ECoG signals of BCI
applications.
2) Enhance the generalizability of the surrogate model
in the proposed MBO method to optimize the DDN
models for decoding EEG/ECoG signals of new unseen
subjects.

II. EEG/ECoG SIGNALS DECODING
The authors of [22] demonstrated a variety of CNN archi-
tectures, ranging from 2- to 31-layer CNN networks, for
decoding the EEG signals in the motor imagery BCI IV 2a
dataset. Furthermore, these deep CNN models could decode
motor imagery EEG signals as well as or better than Filter
Bank Common Spatial Patterns (FBCSP) [23], the winner
of many EEG-decoding competitions. Finally, the Shallow
ConvNet model was the most accurate on average for all
subjects among all the proposed architectures in that work.

The 22-electrode EEG recordings comprising the BCI IV
2a dataset represent nine patients’ motor imagery-related
brain activity. Each individual i has a distinct collection of
EEG recordings made while performing four distinct body
movements, namely ‘‘Left Hand’’, ‘‘Right Hand’’, ‘‘Feet’’ and

B. BCI IV 4 DATASET
The shallow ConvNet model has also been tested on Electro-
physiological (ECoG) signals in the BCI IV 4 dataset showing
better performance than previous works.
The BCI IV 4 dataset consists of 62, 48, and 64 -electrode
ECoG recordings corresponding to 3 epileptic patients. The
dataset has the same structure as the BCI IV 2a. However,
measurements of finger flexion (continuous values) are given
at every timestep instead of class labels (see Figure 4).
C. SHALLOW ConvNet
Shallow ConvNet mimics the spatial filters (bandpass and
Common Spatial Patterns (CSP)) in FBCSP with temporal
and spatial convolution in the initial two layers. A squaring
nonlinearity layer, a mean pooling layer, and a dense layer
with the softmax activation function comprise the remainder
of the pipeline, which resembles the logarithmic variance cal-
culation in FBCSP. Figure 5 illustrates the Shallow ConvNet
model architecture. Although Shallow ConvNet is accurate
enough, its accuracy can be improved further by tuning its
architectural/training parameters.

C. ATTENTION MECHANISM
Our surrogate model leverages the Multi-Head self-Attention
(or intra-attention) mechanism proposed in [25]. Intuitively,
the self-Attention mechanism assesses the semantic relation-
ship between every Shallow ConvNet architectural/training
parameter with every other architectural/training parameter
in Table 1, including itself, by assigning higher weights when
the semantic relationship is stronger and lower weights when
the semantic relationship is weaker and then, re-scaling the
initial parameters’ embeddings according to these weights.
Therefore, the result is eight embedding vectors that carry
contextual information. For example, the ‘‘Temporal Filters’’
parameter in Table 1 would be compared with itself and the
rest (other seven) of the architectural/training parameters to
compute the weights showing its semantic relationship with
the other parameters, and its embedding would be updated by
scaling the embeddings of the other parameters according to
these weights.

A. TRAINING CONFIGURATIONS
1) CONTEXT-LESS APPROACH
Initially, we created three offline datasets, one for the BCI
IV ECoG regression challenge and the other for the BCI
IV 2a EEG high and low-frequency classification chal-
lenges ,5 respectively, to train the surrogate models of our
proposed method. Each dataset consists of 8000 samples,
and each sample results from a different random combina-
tion of the Shallow ConvNet architectural/training parameter
values shown in Table 1. In addition, every sample in the
offline dataset is annotated with its average decoding accu-
racy across all subjects, obtained by re-training the Shallow
ConvNet using the random architecture/training parameters
values associated with that sample itself.
For each offline dataset D, created in the previous step,
we have trained a surrogate model using Algorithm 1 for
approximately 530 epochs. We considered the following
hold-out validation strategies for each test case during our
experiments. Either train on 80% of the configurations in
the offline dataset and validate on the rest 20%, or train

V. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
This paper determines the ideal architectural/training param-
eter values for the Shallow ConvNet model, the state-of-the-
art deep CNN (our baseline model) for decoding the motor
imagery EEG/ECoG signals constituting the BCI IV 2a and
4 datasets, respectively. Our findings demonstrate that we
can choose better settings for the Shallow ConvNet archi-
tectural/training parameters to increase the generalizability
and accuracy of the baseline model. More specifically, when
we excluded one subject, applied the proposed data-driven
optimization method, and then tested the accuracy on that
subject, we obtained an improvement of 14.7% and 61.0%
on the test-set pertinent to the excluded subject in the BCI IV
2a and 4 datasets, respectively. Finally, when we considered
all subjects in our proposed data-driven optimization method,
an average improvement of 5.0% and 31.3% in BCI IV 2a and
4 datasets was achieved, respectively.
The proposed optimization methodology keeps the model
architecture the same, i.e., the number of layers, the type of
layers, and the connectivity between them. Therefore, in the
future, we plan to modify the data-driven offline optimization
methodology to explore the number, the type, and the con-
nectivity of layers, i.e., a Neural Architecture Search (NAS)
algorithm. Moreover, we plan to add hardware implemen-
tation constraints in the proposed optimization algorithm,
e.g., the Peak Memory Usage (PMU) at inference time, the
model size, and the latency to permit the model deploy-
ment on low-cost, battery-operated microcontrollers. Finally,
except for extracting helpful information directly from the
raw EEG/ECoG signals, there are CNN-based analysis tech-
niques that first transform non-stationary signals (such as
EEG/ECoG signals) to the frequency domain, and then CNNs
extract relevant information from these frequency-domain
representations. These kinds of transformations have been
applied successfully in induction motor fault diagnosis [32],
in the automatic detection of speech deficits in cochlear
implant users [33], in phoneme class recognition to extract
phone-attribute features [33] and in the classification of noisy
non-stationary time-series signals for detecting gravitational-
wave signals in intensive real-life [34]. Therefore, one
